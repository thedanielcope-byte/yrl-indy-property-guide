Why FSBO Homes Sell for Less in Indianapolis

The idea of saving on commission is appealing — but the numbers behind for-sale-by-owner sales tell a more complicated story. Here's an honest look at what FSBO sellers gain, what they give up, and what to consider before going it alone.

For Sale By Owner Indianapolis Sellers By Janet Giles-Schultz
For Sale By Owner May 2026 By Janet Giles-Schultz, Principal Broker — Your Realty Link ~8 min read

If you're thinking about selling your Indianapolis home as a for sale by owner — commonly called FSBO — you have a legitimate goal: keep more money in your pocket. That's a completely reasonable thing to want, and you deserve an honest conversation about whether FSBO will actually get you there. Not a sales pitch, not scare tactics — just a straightforward look at where FSBO works, where it typically doesn't, and what the data consistently shows about outcomes for sellers who go it alone versus those who work with a professional. Your Realty Link has helped many FSBO sellers reconsider and ultimately get better outcomes with representation, but we've also had clients who explored FSBO seriously before deciding against it. This article is written for both.

The Appeal of FSBO — We Get It

Let's start with respect. The impulse to sell your own home isn't naive — it comes from a real and reasonable place. You've owned the home for years, you know it better than any agent will, and you can see the commission line on the closing statement and wonder if it's really necessary. In a hot market where homes seem to sell themselves, the case for FSBO looks even stronger.

There's also a sense of control. As a FSBO seller, you set the schedule, you conduct your own showings, you negotiate directly with buyers, and you're not dependent on anyone else's motivation or availability. For sellers who are organized, financially savvy, and have some knowledge of the real estate transaction process, these are genuine advantages.

We're not here to dismiss those instincts. The question isn't whether FSBO can theoretically work — it can, and occasionally does. The question is whether it will likely get you the best financial outcome in the Indianapolis market, when all the real costs and trade-offs are honestly accounted for.

What the Data Shows About FSBO Sale Prices

The most consistent finding in national real estate research is that FSBO homes sell for less than agent-assisted homes — often significantly less. The National Association of Realtors tracks this annually, and year after year the gap is meaningful. FSBO homes have historically sold for tens of thousands of dollars less on average than comparable agent-listed properties.

The explanation isn't that agents have magic powers. It comes down to a few structural realities:

  • Exposure. A MIBOR MLS listing reaches every active buyer's agent in the Indianapolis metro. A FSBO yard sign and a Zillow listing do not. More qualified buyer exposure almost always produces higher offers.
  • Pricing accuracy. Agents who work the market daily have access to current comparable sales data and know how buyers are actually behaving right now. FSBO sellers often price based on emotion, neighborhood rumors, or Zillow estimates — all of which can miss the mark in either direction.
  • Negotiation. Professional negotiation on a home sale is a skill developed over hundreds of transactions. Buyers working with experienced agents know how to structure offers, use inspection findings strategically, and negotiate terms that subtly favor their clients. FSBO sellers negotiating against an experienced buyer's agent are often at a structural disadvantage.

"The commission you're trying to avoid on the sell side almost never exceeds what you lose on the purchase price side. It's a math problem most sellers don't run until after the fact."

— Janet Giles-Schultz, Principal Broker, Your Realty Link

The Hidden Costs FSBO Sellers Underestimate

Even if a FSBO seller prices their home perfectly and markets it well, there are costs built into the transaction that most sellers don't fully account for upfront:

The buyer's agent still gets paid. In almost every FSBO transaction, the buyer is represented by an agent who expects compensation. As a FSBO seller, you'll typically be asked to pay the buyer's agent commission — which means you're not saving the full commission, only the listing agent side. If you're not offering buyer's agent compensation, many agents will steer their clients away from your listing entirely, shrinking your buyer pool.

Legal exposure. Indiana real estate transactions involve disclosure requirements, contract contingencies, title issues, and earnest money procedures that carry real legal risk if handled incorrectly. A mistake in a purchase agreement or a missed disclosure can result in a deal falling apart — or worse, a post-closing dispute. Most FSBO sellers pay a real estate attorney to review documents, which adds cost and doesn't eliminate all risk.

Your time has value. FSBO sellers manage their own showings, respond to buyer inquiries, coordinate with inspectors and appraisers, and track the transaction to close. In a normal sale, an experienced agent handles all of this. If your time is worth anything — and it is — factor that in honestly.

Marketing expenses. Professional photography, listing syndication fees, yard signs, and print materials aren't free. Sellers who try to cut corners here often end up with a listing that looks amateur relative to neighboring agent-listed homes.

Where FSBO Sellers Lose the Most Money

If you had to identify the two points in the FSBO process where sellers most consistently leave money on the table, they are pricing and negotiation — and they're connected.

Pricing mistakes cut in both directions. FSBO sellers who overprice their homes watch days on market accumulate, which signals to buyers that something is wrong. Eventually they reduce the price — often more than they would have needed to with proper pricing from the start. FSBO sellers who underprice (which happens more often than people expect, especially in complex markets) simply leave money behind with no recourse.

Negotiation is where agents earn their commission. When a buyer's agent submits an offer with specific contingencies, timelines, and terms, an experienced listing agent knows immediately what's standard, what's aggressive, and what needs to be pushed back on. FSBO sellers in the same situation are often interpreting these terms for the first time — under time pressure — without a reliable benchmark for what's reasonable.

When FSBO Makes Sense

In the interest of full honesty: there are situations where FSBO can work reasonably well. If you're selling to a known buyer — a family member, a neighbor who's been asking about the house, a coworker who's ready to buy — the transaction is simpler because you've eliminated the buyer search entirely. You still need proper documentation and likely a real estate attorney, but the marketing challenge is gone.

FSBO can also work for sellers who have genuine real estate transaction experience — perhaps a real estate professional themselves, or someone who has bought and sold numerous properties and understands the process deeply. These sellers are equipped to handle what most homeowners encounter only once or twice in a lifetime.

For the vast majority of Indianapolis homeowners selling a primary residence, however, the financial case for full representation is strong. The question isn't whether an agent costs money — it's whether the agent adds more value than they cost. In most well-executed listings, the answer is yes.
