New Construction vs Resale: Which Should You Buy?

A neutral comparison of building new versus buying existing in Central Indiana — including the costs builders don't quote you up front.

📍 Central Indiana 🏗 New vs Existing 🔑 For Buyers

Quick Answer

Choose new construction for a modern layout, warranty coverage, and no deferred maintenance — accepting a longer timeline, a smaller lot, and real extra costs for landscaping, window treatments and fencing. Choose a resale home for mature trees, larger lots, established neighborhoods, faster possession, and more room to negotiate. Both are good choices; they suit different buyers.

This is one of the first real decisions Central Indiana buyers face, and it's often made for the wrong reason — usually because a model home was impressive on a Sunday afternoon. Both paths are legitimate. They just suit different people, budgets, and timelines.

The Case for New Construction

Everything works. No roof at year eight, no furnace of unknown age, no surprise wiring. Most builders include a structural warranty and a shorter workmanship warranty, which genuinely reduces early-ownership risk.

You also get current layouts — open kitchens, larger primary suites, better insulation and windows — and, on a to-be-built home, the ability to choose finishes rather than inherit someone else's taste. Central Indiana has substantial new inventory, particularly in Westfield, McCordsville, Whitestown and Bargersville. See our new construction hub.

The Case for Resale

Location is the big one. Established neighborhoods are established because they're where people wanted to live — closer to employment, with mature trees, sidewalks, and schools that have a track record. New subdivisions are built where land is available, which usually means farther out.

Lots are typically larger, landscaping is grown in, and you can see exactly what you're buying rather than a rendering. You can also move in weeks rather than months, and everything is negotiable — price, repairs, closing costs.

The Costs Nobody Mentions on New Builds

This is where buyers get caught, and it's the most useful part of this page. A base price rarely reflects what you actually spend:

  • Landscaping beyond the builder's minimum — sod, trees, beds
  • Window treatments for an entire house, which is a genuinely large number
  • Fencing, often restricted by HOA rules on style and height
  • Appliances — not always fully included
  • Decks, patios, and finished basements, frequently priced as upgrades
  • Upgrade creep in the design center, where individually reasonable choices compound quickly

Budget for these from the beginning. A resale home usually already has all of it.

Negotiating Differs Completely

Builders resist cutting base price, because a recorded lower sale affects the value of every remaining home in the community. What they will often move on is incentives — closing cost assistance (usually tied to their preferred lender), free upgrades, or appliance packages. Standing inventory nearing the end of a quarter is where the flexibility usually is.

Resale negotiation is broader: price, repairs after inspection, closing costs, possession timing, and included items are all on the table. See negotiating new construction.

Important: the agent in the builder's model home works for the builder. Bring your own representation — it typically costs you nothing and the builder has budgeted for it. See builder representation.

Two Things Buyers Get Wrong

"New construction doesn't need an inspection." It does. New homes are built quickly by many subcontractors, and independent inspectors routinely find missed items — before your warranty period starts running.

"I'll just use the builder's lender." Their incentives can be genuinely good, but compare the whole package. A closing-cost credit paired with a worse rate can cost more over time. Get an outside quote to compare against.

Which Should You Choose?

New construction if you want low maintenance and modern layout, aren't in a rush, and are comfortable being farther out with a smaller lot.

Resale if location and lot size matter most, you need to move soon, you want negotiating room, or you like established neighborhoods with character.

Many buyers tour both and decide quickly once they've stood in a model home and walked a 1990s subdivision the same afternoon. That comparison resolves it faster than any spreadsheet.

Frequently Asked Questions — New Construction vs Resale

Is new construction more expensive than resale?

Often, yes, once you account for the extras. The base price rarely reflects the final cost, because landscaping, window treatments, fencing, appliances, decks and finished basements are frequently additional. A resale home in the same price range usually already includes those, though it may need updates over time.

Do I need a real estate agent to buy new construction?

It is strongly advisable. The agent in the model home represents the builder, not you. Bringing your own representation typically costs you nothing because builders budget for it, and an agent who knows local builders can help with contract terms, inspections and incentive negotiation.

Should I get an inspection on a brand new home?

Yes. New homes are built quickly by many different subcontractors, and independent inspectors routinely find items that were missed. Having them documented before your warranty period begins is far easier than discovering them a year later.

Can you negotiate the price on new construction?

Rarely on base price, because a recorded lower sale affects the value of every remaining home in the community. Builders are usually more flexible on incentives such as closing cost assistance, free upgrades, or appliance packages, particularly on standing inventory near the end of a quarter.
