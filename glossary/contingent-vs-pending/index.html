Contingent vs Pending: What's the Difference?

Two listing statuses that look similar and mean very different things — including whether it's still worth making an offer.

📘 Real Estate Term 🏡 Listing Status 🔑 For Buyers

Quick Answer

A contingent listing has an accepted offer, but conditions — inspection, financing, appraisal — still have to be met. A pending listing has cleared those conditions and is moving toward closing. Practically: contingent deals still fall through with some regularity, while pending deals usually close. If a home you love is contingent, it can still be worth a backup offer.

These two statuses cause more buyer confusion than any other listing terminology — and the difference genuinely matters if you're deciding whether to keep pursuing a house.

Contingent: Accepted, But Not Safe Yet

A contingent listing has an accepted offer with one or more contingencies still outstanding. The buyer might still be completing the inspection, waiting on final loan approval, waiting for the appraisal, or trying to sell their own home.

Any of those can fail. Inspection findings can blow up a deal, financing can be denied in underwriting, an appraisal can come in low, or the buyer's own house may not sell. Contingent means "spoken for, conditionally."

Pending: Nearly Done

Pending means the contingencies have been satisfied or waived and the transaction is heading to closing. Something can still go wrong — a last-minute financing problem, a title issue — but it's much less likely. Most pending sales close.

What This Means If You Want the House

If a home you love goes contingent, it can absolutely still be worth submitting a backup offer. A backup is a formal offer that moves into first position if the current deal collapses. It costs you nothing but paperwork, and buyers do get homes this way.

If it goes pending, the odds drop considerably. It's usually better to redirect your energy — though there's no harm in having your agent ask whether the seller would accept a backup.

Variations You'll See

Listings sometimes carry more specific statuses, such as contingent on the buyer selling their home, or contingent but still accepting showings (often shown as taking backups). These signal how confident the seller is in the deal. A seller still actively showing a contingent home is telling you something useful.

Frequently Asked Questions — Contingent vs pending

Can you still make an offer on a contingent house?

Yes, and it is often worth doing. A backup offer moves into first position if the current deal collapses, which happens with some regularity on contingent listings because inspection, financing, appraisal, or the buyer's own home sale can all fail. It costs nothing but paperwork.

Is pending or contingent closer to sold?

Pending. A contingent listing still has open conditions such as inspection or financing, while a pending listing has cleared those conditions and is moving toward closing. Most pending sales close; contingent deals fall through more often.

How often do contingent deals fall through?

It varies by market and by which contingencies remain, but it is common enough that backup offers are genuinely worthwhile. Financing problems and inspection disputes are the most frequent causes. Deals that are contingent only on a buyer selling their existing home carry the most uncertainty.
