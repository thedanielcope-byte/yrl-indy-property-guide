Moving to Indianapolis: The Complete Relocation Guide

What it costs, where to live, and how to choose — written for people relocating to Central Indiana from somewhere else.

People move to Indianapolis for a fairly consistent set of reasons: housing costs a fraction of what it does on either coast, the job market is more diverse than outsiders expect, and you can drive to a surprising share of the country in a day. What most newcomers underestimate is how different the metro's communities are from one another — choosing the wrong suburb for your commute or your kids' schools is the single most common relocation regret we see.

Why People Move to Indianapolis

Cost of living. This is the headline. Housing in Central Indiana runs well below the national average, and it's the main reason relocating buyers are often shocked by what their budget buys. A household priced out of a starter condo elsewhere can frequently buy a full single-family home here.

A genuinely diverse economy. Indianapolis isn't a one-industry town. Life sciences and pharmaceuticals (Eli Lilly is headquartered here), logistics and distribution, healthcare, insurance, advanced manufacturing, motorsports, and a growing tech sector all have real presence. Salesforce anchors a major downtown tower and the northeast suburbs have built a genuine tech corridor.

Location. Indianapolis sits at the intersection of several interstates and is within a day's drive of a large share of the U.S. population — a big part of why the logistics sector is so strong here, and convenient if family is elsewhere in the Midwest or East.

Manageable scale. The metro is large enough for professional sports, a real arts scene, and a serious restaurant culture, but small enough that a 25-minute commute is considered long.

Remember Indiana property taxes and homeowner's insurance in your monthly math, not just the mortgage. Indiana's constitutional property tax caps keep that portion relatively predictable, and you should file the homestead exemption on your primary residence after closing.

Getting Around

Indianapolis is a driving city. I-465 loops the metro and is the reference point for most directions; I-65, I-69, I-70 and I-74 radiate outward, along with US-31 to the north and US-36 to the west. Which of these serves your commute should heavily influence where you look — a 20-minute commute on the wrong corridor becomes 40.

Indianapolis International Airport (IND) is on the southwest side and is consistently well regarded for how easy it is to use. Public transit exists but is limited compared with larger metros; plan on a car.

Weather, Honestly

Four real seasons. Summers are warm and humid, winters are cold with periodic snow and ice, and spring and fall are genuinely lovely. If you're coming from a milder climate, budget for winter gear and understand that the occasional ice storm is a normal part of the year. Homes here have both central heat and air conditioning as standard.

What Surprises Newcomers

  • How far your money goes. Consistently the biggest reaction from relocating buyers.
  • The roundabouts. Carmel has a famously large number of them. They work, and you get used to them faster than you expect.
  • How seriously youth sports are taken. Grand Park in Westfield is one of the largest facilities of its kind in the country.
  • That "Indy" isn't only the 500. The race is a genuine cultural event, but the city has a substantial arts, food, and trail scene the rest of the year.
  • Property taxes are capped. Indiana's constitutional caps make that line item more predictable than in many states.

Frequently Asked Questions — Moving to Indianapolis

Is Indianapolis a good place to live?

For most people moving here, yes. The main draws are housing costs well below the national average, a diverse economy spanning life sciences, logistics, healthcare and tech, a central location within a day's drive of much of the country, and a metro large enough for real amenities but small enough that a 25-minute commute counts as long. The tradeoffs are real winters and limited public transit.

How much money do you need to move to Indianapolis?

Less than most relocating buyers expect. Homes range from roughly the $120s in small towns to $1 million and up in Carmel and Zionsville, with most suburban family homes falling between the $250s and $600s. Budget 2 to 5 percent of the purchase price for buyer closing costs, plus moving expenses and Indiana property taxes and insurance in your monthly math.

What is the best suburb of Indianapolis to move to?

It depends on your commute and budget. Carmel and Zionsville are the premium choices with top schools; Fishers, Westfield and Noblesville offer strong schools with more value; Greenwood, Avon and Brownsburg are the most affordable of the well-regarded districts. Choose based on which highway corridor serves your daily drive, since that matters more than the rankings.

How long does it take to buy a home when relocating?

Plan roughly 3 to 6 months total. Get pre-approved early, take a scouting trip 2 to 3 months out to tour your shortlist areas back to back, then expect about 30 to 45 days from accepted offer to closing with financing. Compressed timelines are workable with video tours and same-day showing blocks.

Do you need a car in Indianapolis?

Yes, for nearly everyone. Indianapolis is a driving metro built around the I-465 loop and radiating interstates. Public transit exists but is limited compared with larger cities. A few downtown neighborhoods are genuinely walkable for daily needs, but a car is standard.
